About Majuli

Majuli, a verdant and eco-friendly freshwater island nestled in the Brahmaputra River, is a pristine haven just 20 kilometers from Jorhat city in Assam. Encompassing a total area of 1250 square kilometers, Majuli holds the distinction of being the world's largest river island, drawing tourists from far and wide. Its surreal beauty and unique cultural heritage have positioned it as a strong contender for UNESCO's World Heritage Sites. Inhabited mostly by Tribals, Majuli boasts a captivating and diverse culture, earning it the title of the cultural capital of Assam.

Natural Beauty

Majuli, an island dotted with lush green forests, water bodies, and paddy fields, offers not only cultural richness but also breathtaking natural landscapes. Featuring meandering rivers and diverse birdlife, Majuli provides a picturesque backdrop to the local villages and towns. Visitors can immerse themselves in the island's pristine beauty by cycling along scenic trails, enjoying leisurely boat rides on the Brahmaputra River, or relaxing on its picturesque beaches. Known as a birdwatcher's paradise, the island is home to over 200 species of birds, including the rare Greater Adjutant Stork, and its wetlands attract migratory birds during the winter months. There are numerous trekking trails leading to the hilltops, offering panoramic views of the Brahmaputra River and the surrounding landscapes. Additionally, Majuli is a popular destination for adventure sports, with activities such as kayaking, rafting, and paragliding available for visitors.

Cultural Heritage

Majuli serves as the hub of Assamese neo-Vaishnavite culture, is home to several Satras, where visitors can witness traditional dance performances, learn about the unique Vaishnavite philosophy, and marvel at exquisite handcrafted artifacts. The island Additionally, Majuli's tranquil surroundings offer the perfect setting for meditation, yoga, and spiritual rejuvenation, making it an ideal destination for those seeking solace and inner peace. Majuli's festivals, particularly the vibrant Raas festival, are celebrated with joyous fervor, offering visitors a glimpse into its rich traditions and lively spirit.

Ecological Importance

Majuli, shrouded in mist during the monsoon and bathed in golden sunlight during winters, is a geographical marvel and cultural hotspot. Covering around 352 square kilometers, Majuli constantly changes shape due to erosion and sedimentation, yet remains a paradise for nature enthusiasts. The island's economy and culture are deeply tied to fishing, with fishermen casting their nets into the Brahmaputra at dawn. However, erosion threatens Majuli, prompting conservation efforts such as bamboo and geo-textile fencing and tree plantation drives. Sustainable tourism initiatives, including eco-friendly homestays and community-based tourism, aim to preserve the environment and support local livelihoods. Exploring Majuli's villages offers a glimpse into the simplicity and harmony of rural Assamese life while promoting environmental conservation and cultural preservation.

Cultural and Economic Activities in Majuli

The local languages here are Mishing, Assamese and Deori. Agriculture is the main source of income in the town; around 100 different varieties of rice are grown in Majuli. Komal Saul, a unique type of rice is eaten by locals as a breakfast cereal while the Bora Saul is a sticky brown rice used to make Pitha, a traditional cake. Pottery, handloom, boat-making, fishing and dairying are some of the other important economic activities. Face masks and pottery are a must-buy from Majuli.

Restrictions & Conservation Awareness

Religious Sites

Some Satras (Vaishnavite monasteries) on Majuli may have specific entry restrictions based on religious customs. Respect these guidelines when visiting.

Environmental Conservation

Majuli is vulnerable to erosion and environmental degradation. Respect the island's delicate ecosystem and participate in eco-friendly activities during your stay.
