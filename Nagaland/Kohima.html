Kohima City

A land ruled by the Nagas

Overview

Kohima, the capital of Nagaland, is a picturesque hill station known for its stunning landscapes and rich cultural heritage. Situated at an altitude of 1500 meters, Kohima boasts of lush green hills, dense forests, and a serene environment, making it a paradise for nature lovers and adventure enthusiasts. The name 'Kohima' is derived from the local Kewhi flowers, which are found abundantly in the region. Built by the British, Kohima is home to the Naga and Kulkis tribes, known for their vibrant and mystical culture. The city has a rich history, having been the site of intense fighting during World War II, as part of a 64-day battle with the Japanese. Today, Kohima is a blend of modernity and tradition, offering visitors a glimpse into its past through attractions like the Commonwealth War Cemetery, which houses thousands of soldiers from the war. Additionally, Kohima offers a range of attractions including museums, zoos, sanctuaries, and self-governing villages, making it a fascinating destination for travelers.

History

Kohima was initially inhabited by the Angami Naga tribes, one of the largest of its kind. In around 1840, the British started the incursions to take on the territory which was met by stringent resistance by the freedom loving Nagas, who had never been conquered by any empire before. For this fact, the British took around four decades to capture the comparatively tiny territory. Kohima was the first seat of British modern administration as the headquarters of Naga Hills District in 1879. It has also been etched in history for the infamous Battle of Kohima during the Burma Campaign in 1944 at the time of World War 2. It was a 64-day long war with the Japanese who eventually lost to the allies. Afterwards, post-independence, when Nagaland became a full-fledged state in 1963, Kohima was made its official capital.

Places to Visit

Some of the popular tourist attractions in Kohima include the Kohima War Cemetery, Kohima Zoo, Dzukou Valley, and the Nagaland State Museum. The city is also known for its vibrant markets and traditional Naga cuisine.

Kohima Zoo

The Kohima Zoological Park, nestled in the scenic hills of Kohima, Nagaland, is a haven for wildlife enthusiasts and nature lovers. Spanning over an area of lush greenery, the zoo is dedicated to the conservation of local flora and fauna while providing an educational experience for visitors. The park's collection includes a variety of indigenous species, showcasing the rich biodiversity of the region. Visitors can spot rare and endangered animals such as the Himalayan black bear, leopard cat, and Blyth's tragopan, along with several species of deer. Apart from its diverse animal population, the Kohima Zoo also boasts a botanical garden that features a wide array of plant species native to Nagaland. The zoo's well-maintained habitats and naturalistic enclosures provide a comfortable environment for the animals while offering visitors a glimpse into the region's natural heritage. With its tranquil surroundings and educational exhibits, the Kohima Zoo is a must-visit destination for anyone looking to experience the beauty of Nagaland's wildlife and ecosystems.

Nagaland State Mueseum

The Nagaland State Museum, established in 1970 in Kohima, is a captivating institution preserving the cultural heritage of Nagaland's indigenous tribes. It serves as a window into the rich and diverse traditions of the Naga people, showcasing a plethora of artifacts ranging from traditional attire and jewelry to weapons, musical instruments, and household items. These exhibits offer a deep insight into the lifestyle, beliefs, and customs of the Naga tribes, highlighting their unique cultural identity. The museum's collection extends beyond material culture to include photographs, documents, and audio-visual presentations, providing a comprehensive overview of Nagaland's history and cultural evolution. Additionally, the museum features a natural history section that displays the region's rich biodiversity, including taxidermy specimens and preserved plant specimens. The Nagaland State Museum stands as a testament to the state's vibrant cultural tapestry and is a must-visit for those eager to explore the cultural and natural heritage of Nagaland.

Kohima War Cemetery

The Kohima War Cemetery, located in Kohima, Nagaland, is a poignant reminder of the fierce Battle of Kohima fought during World War II. This cemetery is dedicated to the soldiers of the Allied forces who made the ultimate sacrifice in defending Kohima against the Japanese invasion. The cemetery, maintained by the Commonwealth War Graves Commission, is a beautifully landscaped memorial that honors over 1,420 soldiers who lost their lives in the battle. The Kohima War Cemetery is known for its serene and peaceful atmosphere, offering visitors a place for quiet reflection and remembrance. The cemetery's well-manicured lawns and carefully tended flower beds create a solemn yet picturesque setting. The memorial is marked by a stone inscription that reads: "When You Go Home, Tell Them Of Us And Say, For Their Tomorrow, We Gave Our Today." Visiting the Kohima War Cemetery is a moving experience, highlighting the bravery and sacrifice of those who fought in one of the most significant battles of World War II.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Kohima is during the winter months, from November to February, when the weather is cool and pleasant, making it ideal for exploring the city and its surroundings. The temperatures during this time range from a comfortable 10°C to 25°C, making it perfect for outdoor activities and sightseeing. Additionally, this period coincides with the Hornbill Festival, held in the first week of December, which is a great opportunity to experience Naga culture and traditions. However, it's essential to check the festival dates and make accommodation arrangements well in advance, as this is a popular time for tourists to visit Kohima.

Location

Kohima, situated in the south at an altitude of 1444m above sea level, occupies pride of place as the capital city of Nagaland. Sharing its borders with Dimapur and Peren District in the West, Zunheboto and Phek District in the East, Manipur State in the South and Tseminyu District in the North. One of the oldest among the eleven districts of the state, Kohima is the first seat of modern administration as the Headquarters of Naga Hills District (then under Assam). When Nagaland became a full fledged state on 1st December, 1963, Kohima was christened as the capital of the state.
